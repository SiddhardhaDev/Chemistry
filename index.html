Hydrocarbons & Alkanes Unveiled!

Your ultimate guide to understanding the fascinating world of carbon and hydrogen compounds.

What are Hydrocarbons?

Hydrocarbons are simply compounds made up of only carbon (C) and hydrogen (H) atoms. They're the building blocks for so much around us!

Everyday Examples:

  • LPG: Liquefied Petroleum Gas – a common domestic fuel, known for minimal pollution.
  • CNG: Compressed Natural Gas.
  • LNG: Liquefied Natural Gas.
  • Petrol, Diesel, Kerosene: Obtained from crude petroleum through a process called fractional distillation.
  • Coal Gas: Produced from the destructive distillation of coal.

Beyond fuels, hydrocarbons are crucial for making polymers (like polythene, polypropene, polystyrene), used as solvents for paints, and act as starting materials for many dyes and drugs (think Benzene, Xylene).

Classification of Hydrocarbons:

Hydrocarbons are broadly classified into two main types:

  1. Aliphatic Hydrocarbons: These are "open-chain" hydrocarbons.
    • Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes): All carbon valences are satisfied by single bonds. They are also called paraffins.

      Methane (CH4)

      Ethane (C2H6)

    • Unsaturated Hydrocarbons (Alkenes & Alkynes): Contain carbon-carbon double or triple bonds.

      Ethylene (Ethene, CH2=CH2)

      Acetylene (Ethyne, CH≡CH)

  2. Aromatic Hydrocarbons: Cyclic compounds obeying Huckel's rule ((4n+2)π electrons).

    Benzene (C6H6)

    Toluene (C7H8)

Alkanes: The Saturated Stars

Alkanes are the simplest family of hydrocarbons. They're all about single bonds!

  • General Formula: CnH2n+2 (where 'n' is the number of carbon atoms).
  • Structure: Carbon atoms in alkanes undergo sp3 hybridization, giving them a tetrahedral geometry. The H-C-H bond angle is a perfect 109°28'.
  • Bonds: Only contain carbon-carbon (C-C) and carbon-hydrogen (C-H) sigma (σ) bonds. No double or triple bonds here!
  • Inertness: Under normal conditions, alkanes are quite unreactive – they're "paraffins" (little affinity). They don't easily react with acids, bases, or most reagents.
  • Methane (CH4): The very first member, often found in coal mines and marshy areas.

Making Alkanes: Synthesis Secrets!

I. Catalytic Reduction of Alkenes/Alkynes (Hydrogenation)

+

Turning unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes and alkynes) into saturated alkanes by adding hydrogen (H2).

The Reaction:

Example: R-CH=CH-R' + H2 ⟶ R-CH2-CH2-R'

  • Catalysts: Platinum (Pt), Palladium (Pd), Nickel (Ni), or PtO2 (Adam's catalyst).
  • Conditions: Pt/Pd work at room temperature. Ni needs higher temperatures (200°-300°C).
  • Sabatier-Senderen's Reduction: Specific name when Nickel is the catalyst.
  • Mechanism: Primarily heterogeneous catalysis (reaction on catalyst surface) leading to cis addition of hydrogen.
  • Homogeneous Catalysis: Can also occur with catalysts like Wilkinson's catalyst (Chlorotris(triphenylphosphine) Rhodium(I)). This catalyst is selective!
  • Cannot prepare: Methane (CH4).

II. Reduction of Alkyl Halides

+

Converting alkyl halides (R-X) into alkanes (R-H).

Key Methods:

  • Using Zn-Cu couple and ethyl alcohol (C2H5OH).
  • Using Zn with CH3COOH, dil. HCl, or NaOH.
  • Using Metal Hydrides:
    • LiAlH4 (Lithium Aluminium Hydride): Reduces primary & secondary halides (tertiary give alkenes).
    • NaBH4 (Sodium Borohydride): Reduces secondary & tertiary halides (not primary).
    • Ph3SnH (Triphenyl tin hydride): Reduces ALL three types!
  • Catalytic Hydrogenolysis: With Pd-C catalyst for iodides, 3° chlorides/bromides.
  • Conc. HI & Red Phosphorus: Very powerful! Reduces iodides, alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, and even acids to alkanes.

    Example: R-I + 2HI red P150°C ⟶ R-H + I2

III. Hydrolysis of Alkyl Magnesium Halide (Grignard Reagent)

+

Grignard reagents (RMgX) react with water or dilute HCl to yield alkanes.

The Reaction:

Example: RMgX + H2O ⟶ R-H + Mg(OH)X

  • Methane (CH4): Can be made by hydrolyzing methyl magnesium bromide/iodide, or by hydrolyzing beryllium carbide (Be2C) or aluminium carbide (Al4C3).

IV. Decarboxylation of Fatty Acids

+

Heating the sodium salt of an alkanoic acid with soda lime (CaO + NaOH) gives an alkane with one less carbon atom.

The Reaction:

R-COONa + NaOH CaOΔ ⟶ R-H + Na2CO3

  • Chain Shortening: The resulting alkane has one carbon less than the original acid.
  • Soda Lime (CaO + NaOH): CaO acts as a water-absorbing agent.
  • Limitation: Not a generally useful method for preparation.

V. Wurtz Reaction

+

Alkyl halides react with sodium metal in the presence of dry ether to form alkanes.

The Reaction:

R-X + 2Na + X-R Dry etherΔ ⟶ R-R + 2NaX

  • Mechanism: Can proceed via ionic or free radical pathways.
  • Limitations:
    • Fails for tertiary alkyl halides (forms alkenes).
    • Not suitable for odd-numbered carbon alkanes (due to mixed products if different halides are used).
    • Cannot prepare Methane (CH4).
  • Wurtz-Fittig Reaction: A variation using haloarenes and alkyl halides.

VI. Frankland's Reaction

+

Similar to Wurtz, but uses Zinc (Zn) metal instead of Sodium (Na).

The Reaction:

R-X + Zn + X-R Dry etherΔ ⟶ R-R + ZnX2

  • Both Wurtz & Frankland are best for alkanes with an even number of carbon atoms.

Corey-House Synthesis

+

A versatile method for preparing alkanes, often giving better control over the product than Wurtz reaction.

Key Steps:

  1. Alkyl halide with Lithium in ether forms alkyllithium (CH3Br + 2Li ⟶ CH3Li + LiBr).
  2. Alkyllithium reacts with Cuprous Iodide (CuI) to form Lithium Dialkylcuprate (I), also known as Gilman's reagent (2CH3Li + CuI ⟶ Li(CH3)2Cu + LiI).
  3. Gilman's reagent then reacts with another alkyl halide to produce the alkane (CH3CH2Br + Li(CH3)2Cu ⟶ CH3-CH2-CH3 + CH3Cu + LiBr).

VII. Kolbe's Electrolysis Method (Decarboxylation)

+

Electrolysis of a concentrated aqueous solution of an alkali (Na or K) salt of a saturated monocarboxylic acid using Pt electrodes produces alkanes.

The Reaction:

2RCOONa + 2H2O ⟶ R-R + 2CO2 + 2NaOH + H2

  • Alkanes with an even number of carbons are formed at the anode.
  • Involves a free radical mechanism at the anode.
  • Cannot prepare: Methane (CH4).

VIII. Wolff-Kishner Reduction

+

This reaction converts the carbonyl group (>C=O) of aldehydes and ketones into a methylene group (>CH2), reducing them to alkanes.

The Reaction:

Aldehyde/Ketone H2N.NH2 (Hydrazine) / C2H5ONa or NaOH (180°C) ⟶ Alkane + N2

Individual Methods for Methane (CH4)

+
  • From CO: Carbon monoxide (CO) + Hydrogen (3H2) over Ni (200°-300°C) ⟶ CH4 + H2O.
  • From CO2: Carbon dioxide (CO2) + Hydrogen (4H2) over Ni (200°-300°C) ⟶ CH4 + 2H2O.
  • Also from hydrolysis of Beryllium carbide (Be2C) or Aluminum carbide (Al4C3) (as mentioned in Grignard section).

Other Interesting Reactions!

+
  • Alkyl Amine + Grignard Reagent: R-NH2 + R'MgX ⟶ R'-H + R-NHMgX
  • Alcohol + Grignard Reagent (Zerewitinoff's Reaction): R'-OH + RMgX ⟶ R-H + R'-OMgX

Alkanes: What They're Like (Physical Properties)

  • Nonpolar: Due to covalent C-C and C-H bonds.
  • Low Boiling Points: Because of weak van der Waals forces.
  • States at 298K:
    • C1-C4 are gases.
    • C5-C17 are liquids.
    • Above C17 are solids.
  • Density: Lighter than water. All are heavier than air, except methane (CH4).
  • Boiling Point Trends:
    • Increases with increasing molecular weight.
    • For isomers, more branches = lower boiling point (less surface area for intermolecular forces).

      Example: Pentane > Isopentane > Neopentane

  • Melting Point Trends:
    • Alkanes with even 'C' atoms often have higher melting points due to better packing.
    • More spherical shape = highest melting point among isomers.

      Example: 2,2-dimethylpropane (Neopentane) has a higher MP than n-pentane or isopentane.

  • Solubility: Soluble in organic solvents. Solubility decreases with increasing molecular weight.

Alkanes in Action: Chemical Properties!

Though stable, alkanes undergo substitution reactions under specific conditions.

I. Halogenation (Free Radical Mechanism!)

+

Hydrogen atoms are replaced by halogen atoms (Cl2, Br2).

Conditions:

  • Sunlight or UV light, OR
  • High temperatures (573K-773K) in the dark.
  • Forms a mixture of halogen derivatives.

Reactivity Orders:

  • Halogen Reactivity: F2 > Cl2 > Br2 > I2
    • Fluorination: Explosive, so F2 is diluted.
    • Iodination: Very slow and reversible; requires oxidizing agents (HNO3, HIO3) to remove HI.
  • Hydrogen Reactivity in Alkane: 3°H > 2°H > 1°H (tertiary > secondary > primary)

Mechanism: Free Radical Chain Reaction

  1. Chain Initiation: Halogen molecule splits into free radicals (Cl-Cl hν ⟶ 2Cl•).
  2. Chain Propagation: Radicals react, forming new radicals and products (CH4 + Cl• ⟶ CH3• + HCl; CH3• + Cl2 ⟶ CH3Cl + Cl•).
  3. Chain Termination: Radicals combine to form stable molecules (CH3• + CH3• ⟶ C2H6; CH3• + Cl• ⟶ CH3Cl; Cl• + Cl• ⟶ Cl2).

II. Combustion (Burning for Energy!)

+

Alkanes burn in excess oxygen to produce carbon dioxide, water, and a lot of heat (why they are fuels!).

General Reaction:

CnH2n+2 + (&frac32;n+½)O2 ⟶ nCO2 + (n+1)H2O + Energy

Example: CH4 + 2O2 ⟶ CO2 + 2H2O + 890 KJ mole-1

Incomplete Combustion:

With insufficient oxygen, alkanes can form carbon black (soot).

CH4 + O2 ⟶ C + 2H2O

  • Carbon black is used in printer ink, black pigments, and filters.

III. Controlled Oxidation

+

Partial oxidation of alkanes to form other compounds under specific conditions.

  • To Methanol: Methane (CH4) + Oxygen (O2) via copper tube (523K, 100 atm) ⟶ 2CH3OH (Methanol).
  • To Methanal (Formaldehyde): Methane (CH4) + Oxygen (O2) over heated molybdenum oxide (Mo2O3) ⟶ HCHO + H2O (Methanal).

IV. Isomerization

+

Converting straight-chain (n-alkanes) into branched-chain alkanes.

Conditions:

  • Heating with anhydrous aluminum chloride (AlCl3) and hydrogen chloride (HCl).

Example: CH3CH2CH2CH3 (n-butane) AlCl3/HCl ⟶ CH3CH(CH3)CH3 (isobutane)

V. Aromatization or Catalytic Reforming

+

Transforming alkanes (min. 6 carbons) into aromatic compounds.

Conditions:

  • Heating at 773K and 10-20 atm pressure.
  • Catalysts: Chromium, Molybdenum, or Vanadium oxides.

Example: n-heptane Cr2O3/Al2O3, 773K ⟶ Methyl benzene (Toluene)

VI. Reaction with Steam

+

Methane (CH4) reacts with steam (H2O) to produce carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H2).

Conditions:

  • 1000°C in the presence of a nickel (Ni) catalyst.

Reaction: CH4 + H2O Ni, 1000°C ⟶ CO + 3H2

VII. Pyrolysis (Cracking)

+

Decomposition of higher alkanes into smaller fragments by strong heating in the absence of air. It's a free radical reaction.

  • Important for producing petrol, diesel from kerosene oil or crude oil.

Example: CH4 1000°C ⟶ C + 2H2

Example: C2H6 450°C ⟶ C2H4 + H2

Beyond the Lab: Uses of Hydrocarbons!

  • Fuels: Petrol, diesel, kerosene, LPG, CNG, LNG, coal gas.
  • Polymers: Starting materials for polythene, polypropene, polystyrene.
  • Solvents: Higher hydrocarbons are used as paint solvents.
  • Dyes & Drugs: Important starting materials (e.g., Benzene, Xylene).
  • Specific Compounds:
    • CCl4 (Carbon Tetrachloride / Pyrene): Fire extinguisher.
    • C2Cl6: Artificial camphor.
    • CCl2F2 (Freon / Dichlorodifluoromethane): Refrigerant.
    • CF2Cl2 (CFC-11 / Chlorotrifluoromethane): Refrigerant and in aerosol sprays.
